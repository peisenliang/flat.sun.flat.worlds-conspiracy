Article #1: The Truth

"Believe the Truth we Bestow." The official heliocentric model claims the Sun is a near-perfect plasma sphere, 1.39 million km in diameter, 149.6 million km away, powered by nuclear fusion. But repeatable measurements, geometry, and atmospheric observations suggest a different reality: a close, flat, disk-shaped Sun above a planar Earth.

Light Falloff & the Inverse-Square Discrepancy

Officially, light intensity I decreases with the square of distance: I ∝ 1/r². From equator to 66° latitude, irradiance should drop roughly by I × cos(θ) plus atmospheric loss.

Observed:
Equator average = 1361 W/m²
66° latitude summer peak ≈ 550 W/m²

Predicted (official) ≈ 230–300 W/m²

This slower drop-off fits better with a closer, flat Sun where ray divergence is smaller.

Angular Diameter Constancy

With a spherical Sun, distance varies by about 5 million km yearly, so angular diameter should change by Δθ ≈ (Δd / d) × 100% = (5×10⁶ / 1.496×10⁸) × 100% ≈ 3.34%

Expected angular diameter in arcminutes varies from ~32.53′ to ~33.62′. Observed solar photography shows negligible variance within ±0.05′ — consistent with a fixed-distance flat Sun.

High-Altitude Light Divergence

If the Sun were 149.6 million km away, sunlight rays should be essentially parallel (<0.01° divergence). At 20 km altitude, sunrise/sunset timelapse shows rays diverging by about 6–12°, implying a light source only a few thousand km above Earth.

Limb Darkening = Edge Foreshortening

Spherical model explains limb darkening as cooler upper layers of a gaseous sphere. Disk model replicates this effect with laboratory halogen disks due to edge-angle emission alone.

Other Observations

  • Thermal Distribution Deviations: Localized hotspots resemble a nearby overhead Sun.
  • Shadow Length Inconsistencies: Recalculations using a flat Sun give consistent results.
  • Polar Midnight Sun Angle Mismatch: Vertical oscillations of about 2° fit a nearby Sun.
  • Solar Transit Timing Anomalies: Sunrise takes 4–5 minutes at high latitudes, implying a closer Sun.
